Understanding How camera captures images: Parts of the Camera: Still or DSLR Camera


Lens


  • Aperture / F-Stop / Iris
  • Focal length (Wide / Telephoto / Zoom)
  • Focus
  • Filter (Neutral Density) These are placed in front of lens. Pro Video cameras have them built-in.
  • Shutter
  • Shutter speed
  • Image Sensor (Film)
  • Sensitivity / Gain / ISO
  • White Balance
  • Viewfinder/Display
  • Film movie cameras and DSLRs have optical viewfinders
  • Digital cameras, Video, DSLR, Mirrorless etc. have display screens and some have digital viewfinders.
  • Focus

    • Focal Length


  • Lenses come in various focal lengths. From Ultra Wide Angle (fish-eye) to Super Telephoto (think telescope)
  • Regardless of focal length, there are 2 types.
  • Zoom: where the focal length can change
  • Prime: fixed focal length
  • Focal length is described in millimetres. The smaller the number, the wider the angle. The larger the mm (say 200mm) the more narrow the angle of view: telephoto.
  • Focal length plays as strong a roll in creating visual media as lighting does. Maybe more so. If you want to shoot a person in a Medium Shot (From stomach up) you could set up the camera very close with a wide lens, or set up 20 feet away and use a telephoto lens. While the size of the subject in the frame remains the same, everything else changes. Particularly the subjects relation to the background...or their environment.


    • Light


  • Of course, the most obvious thing to change exposure is add or remove light. Either natural light (direct sunlight, or shoot in shade, under cloudy sky, etc.) or using lighting instruments. Bigger Brighter lights, or dim the lights.
  • Aperture
  • This is a physical part of a lens, and controls the amount of light coming through much the way a water faucet handle controls the amount of water coming through a faucet.
  • Small Aperture: Less light, more depth of field
  • Large aperture: More light comes in, but shallow depth of field.
  • It is referred to by many names
  • Iris
  • f/stop: The f-stop is the actual number used to define the opening. Smaller number:Wider open
  • Maximum (maximum open, or smallest number) is dependent on the physical design of the lens.

    • Gain / ISO


  • Video Cameras use GAIN, measured starting at 0 dB, moving up in increments of 3 to double sensitivity. (+3dB, +6dB, =9dB, etc.)
  • DSLR and Mirrorless cameras use ISO. The base here is usually ISO 100.
  • This is the sensitivity of the image sensor to collecting light. (Or the speed of film in a film camera)
  • The more gain, or the higher the Gain or ISO, the more sensitive to light the sensor becomes, allowing you to shoot with less light. TRADE-OFF: the noisier the image becomes. (Or more grainy with film)
  • The lower the Gain or ISO, the more defined and clean the image. TRADE-OFF: less light sensitive, requiring more light for proper exposure.

    • Other Factors

  • Neutral Density
  • Shutter Speed
  • The longer time the sensor is exposed to light, the more light it can collect. This happens in a very short amount of time. If you are shooting at 30 fps (Frames Per Second) each frame can only be exposed for 1/30 of a second or less.
